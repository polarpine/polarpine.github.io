    Computers and Transistors

    By Polarpine August 17, 2014 Tweet Like +1

    Today’s subject began as a project to explain how computers work and now as I’ve danced down the rabbit hole, will find us somewhere between memory, the binary numeral system, transistors and a touch of silicon. To fully explain one segment of a computer would take pages and pages and hours and hours. I’m hopefully going to skim through some of it and delve into enough to keep it understandable and interesting and not pull us too deep into onbscurity.

    A computer is an electronic device for processing and storing data. Computers can do a set of simple logical and arithmetic functions, namely: addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and comparisons (eg. greater than, equal to, less than or equal to). They perform these functions according to a determined program on user input. One of the many great things about computers is their programmability. Users can add or change programs and manipulate a computer to do many new things, as opposed to a calculater (which is an electronic processor) which has little to no memory and ability to change it’s functions.

    Okay, so computers have memory. Let’s first talk about how computers view the world.

    Yep. Ones and Zeros. This probably isn’t surpise to many people these days. But what may be a bit more elusive is what this means. Computers use a base-2 numbering system. We humans chose to use a base-10 numbering system or decimal. A base-10 number system holds 10 digits or characters in a column before moving on (or carrying over) to the next column. What do I mean by this? Well, to express the number 12 in base-10 (beyond just typing 12 as I just did) one needs a 1 in the “tens column” and a two in the “ones column”, translating into one ten plus two ones, or twelve. In base 10, the columns follow right to left: ones, tens, hundreds, thousands, etc.

    12

    In base-2 systems we have two characters to work with and hold in a column before spilling over into the next column. This gives us the columns (again moving right to left - smaller to bigger): ones, twos, fours, eights, sixteens, thirty-twos etc.

    So how would we convert 12 written in base-10 to the numeric representation in base-2? We start with the largest complete column (fully filled), then move down filling as needed. To make twelve, I’ll need one from the eights column, one from the fours column, nothing from the twos column and nothing from the ones column.

    1100

    You shoud be able to see that any number that can be written in base-10 could also be written in base-2. But further than this, a computer can function with the world while operating in a base-2 system. Meaning, a computer, given the key to translate, can do all of it’s calculations and storage in base-2 and still communicate with someone or something in base-10, or images, or frequencies.

    How does a computer know what a one or zero is in the first place? It doesn’t. In the truest since, it does not fundamentally need to recognize ones and zeros. It does need to recognize two distince states. Remember that ones and zeros are ultimately just symbols or characters, in binary numerals, the two characters represent yes something (1) in this column or no, not something (0) in this column. The whole world can be boiled down to a series of yeses and nos, or zeros and ones.

    So how does a computer know about yeses and nos? Enter the transistor.

    The transistor is one of the greatest inventions/advancements of the last century. It may not be at the tip of people’s tongues like microwave ovens, duct tape and the slinky, but the invention of the transistor paved the way for computers and electronics as we know them today.

    A transistor is a semi-conductor device used to amplify or switch electronic signals. This means we have a small, electronic device that can exist in an on or off position. Or….yes or no….or zero or one! It’s a mechanical device that can speak the language of computers AND is compact and reliable. This last statement refers to the original vacuum tubes used in computing that required lots of energy, space and cooling. Transistors were and are a huge advancement allowing computers to become personal, or personal computers. So small you could have one in your pocket!

    To recap, we have a small (very small!) device that can hold a position of zero or one. Be streamed together and function with little power supplied. A language that functions entirely in zeros and ones, a way to translate that language to our macro world. We have hard memory or non-volatile memory. This is memory that is in essence written down. It does not need a power supply to hold a zero or one position. An example of this is a CD. If you were to look closely enough at a CD, you would see a series of dark or light lines resonating out from the center of a disc. It’s most noticable when you look at a freshly burned disc. The tightly packed dark and light spaces are read by a computer scanner as zeros and ones. A CD doesn’t need power to maintain it’s writtten data.

    Putting all of this together, we begin to see the framework of a computer. The language it understands and where it holds it’s understanding.

    This should all serve as a primer for anyone really intersted in how computers work. In future posts I’d like to delve deeper into the memory aspects of computers and explain how transistors work.
